Consider the rotation group \begin{aligned} \calM = \SOn = \{X \in \Rnn \colon X^\top X = I_n, \det(X) = 1\} \end{aligned}

Show that $\calM$ is an embedded submanifold of $\calE = \Rnn$. What is the dimension of $\calM$ ? What are the tangent spaces of $\calM$ ? What is the relationship between $\Stnp$ and $\SOn$ ?

Observe that $\SOn = \text{St}(n,n) \cap (\det^{-1}(\{-1\}))^c$. As $(\det^{-1}(\{-1\}))^c$ is open we have that $\SOn$ is a open subset of $\text{St}(n,n)$. Thus by the exercise Smooth embedded submanifolds of smooth embedded subamnifolds we have that $\SOn$ is an embedded submanifold with the same dimension than $\text{St}(n,n)$, i.e., $n(n-\frac{n+1}{2})=n(n-1)/2$ and the same tangent spaces, i.e., for all $X \in \SOn$ we have that $\T_X \SOn = \{V \in \reals^{n \times n} \; | \; X^\top V = -V^\top X\}$.

The matrix exponential $\exp \colon \reals^{n \times n} \to \reals^{n \times n}$ is the smooth function defined by $$\exp(A) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} A^k, \quad \quad \text{where $A^0 = I$}.$$ Convergence of the infinite sum can be made rigorous, but we do not concern ourselves with that here.

Let $\Omega \in \reals^{n \times n}$ be skew-symmetric, $\Omega = -\Omega^\top$. Show that $\exp(\Omega) \in \calM$, where $\exp$ denotes the matrix exponential. Use the following two properties of the matrix exponential: (a) $\exp(A)^\top = \exp(A^\top)$ for all $A \in \reals^{n \times n}$; (b) $\exp(A) \exp(B) = \exp(A + B)$ if $A, B$ commute. To show that determinant is positive use an argument about connectedness.

Using the two properties from the hint, \begin{aligned} \exp(\Omega)^\top \exp(\Omega) = \exp(-\Omega) \exp(\Omega) = I_n. \end{aligned} This proves already that $\exp(\Omega) \in \SOn$. As the determinant of $\exp(\Omega)$ is either $-1$ or $1$, we only need to show that the determinant is positive.

Clearly the map $A \in \reals^{n \times n} \mapsto \det(\exp(A))$ is continuous and the set $\text{Skew}(n) := \{\Omega \in \reals^{n \times n} \; | \; \Omega = - \Omega^\top\}$ is star shaped as for $\Omega \in \text{Skew}(n)$ we have that $t \cdot \Omega \in \text{Skew}(n)$ for all $t \in [0,1]$. As a star shaped map is in particular connected we get that $\det(\exp(\text{Skew}(n))) \subseteq \{-1,1\}$ is connected. Thus $\det(\exp(.))$ has to be constant on $\text{Skew}(n)$. Finally, as $\det(\exp(0))=1$ we get that $\det(\exp(\Omega))=1$ for all $\Omega \in \text{Skew}(n)$.

Let $\Omega \in \reals^{n \times n}$ be skew-symmetric. Show that $\frac{d}{d t}[\exp(t \Omega)]_{t = 0} = \Omega$. When taking the derivative of the exponential map, feel free to manipulate the infinite sum without worrying about convergence (this can be made rigorous).

We have that $\exp(t\Omega) - I_n = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} (t \Omega)^k = t \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{t^{k-1}}{k!} \Omega^k$.

Thus we have that \begin{aligned} &\left \| \frac{\exp(t \Omega) - \exp(0)}{t} - \Omega \right \| = \left \| \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{t^{k-1}}{k!} \Omega^k - \Omega \right \| = \left \| \sum_{k=2}^\infty \frac{t^{k-1}}{k!} \Omega^k \right \| = |t| \left \| \sum_{k=2}^\infty \frac{t^{k-2}}{k!} \Omega^k \right \| \\ &\leq |t| \sum_{k=2}^\infty \frac{|t|^{k-2}}{k!} ||\Omega||^k = |t| ||\Omega||^2 \sum_{k=2}^\infty \frac{|t|^{k-2}}{k!} ||\Omega||^{k-2} \leq |t| ||\Omega||^2 \exp(|t| ||\Omega||) \xrightarrow[]{t \to 0} 0. \end{aligned}

Define $\Retr_X(V) = X \exp(X^\top V)$. Show that $\Retr_X(V) \in \calM$ for all $(X, V) \in \T\calM$. We know that $X^\top V$ is skew-symmetric, so $\exp(X^\top V) \in \calM$ by part 2. Hence, $X \exp(X^\top V) \in \calM$. Show that $\Retr \colon \T\calM \to \calM$ is a retraction. Use without proof that the $\exp$ map is smooth on $\reals^{n \times n}$.

The map $(X,V) \in \reals^{n \times n} \times \reals^{n \times n} \mapsto X \exp(X^\top V) \in \reals^{n \times n}$ is smooth as a composition of smooth maps. Thus $R: T \calM \to \calM$ is indeed well defined by part 4 and smooth.

Further, $R_X(0)=X$ and using part 3, we have $$\frac{d}{dt}[\Retr_X(t V)]_{t= 0} = X \frac{d}{dt}[\exp(t X^\top V)]_{t=0} = X X^\top V = V.$$

(Optional) For $X \in \calM$, is $\Retr_X \colon \T_X\calM \to \calM$ injective ? Try to construct a nonzero $2 \times 2$ skew-symmetric matrix whose exponential is identity.

Observe that $\Omega \in \text{Skew}(2)$ is equivalent to $\Omega = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & x \\ -x & 0 \end{bmatrix} = x \cdot N$ with $N = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ for some $x \in \reals$. Further, observe that $N^2 = - I_2$, $N^3 = - N$ and $N^4 = I_2$. Thus we have that \begin{aligned} \sum_{k=0}^{2n} \frac{\Omega^k}{k!} = \begin{bmatrix} \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{(-1)^k x^{2k}}{(2k)!} & \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{(-1)^k x^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} \\ -\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{(-1)^k x^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} & \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{(-1)^k x^{2k}}{(2k)!} \end{bmatrix}, \end{aligned} showing that $\exp(\Omega) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(x) & \sin(x) \\ -\sin(x) & \cos(x) \end{bmatrix}$.

Thus taking $\Omega = 2 \pi \cdot N$ we get that $\exp(\Omega) = I_2$. Now, let $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n \geq 2$ and define the matrix $\Omega = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \pi \cdot N & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.$ Then clearly $\Omega \in \text{Skew}(n)$ and $\exp(\Omega)=I_n$.

Let $X \in \SOn$ and take $V = X \Omega$ with $\Omega$ as above. Then $V \in T_X \SOn$ and \begin{aligned} R_X(V) = X \exp(X^\top V) = X \exp(\Omega) = X, \end{aligned} giving that $R_X$ isn't injective.

This shows that $\forall X \in \SOn$ we have that $R_X$ isn't injective.
