Let $\calM$ be a Riemannian manifold and $c \colon \reals \to \calM$ be a curve. Recall that $c$ is a geodesic if its (intrinsic) acceleration is zero: $c''(t) = 0$ for all $t \in \reals$. Let $\calM = \Sd$ be the $(d-1)$-dimensional sphere embedded in $\reals^d$ with the usual Riemannian metric. For $(x, v) \in \T \calM$ with $||v||=1$ consider \begin{aligned} c(t) = \cos(t) x + \sin(t) \frac{v}{\|v\|}. \end{aligned}

Show that the curve $c$ given by in the above equation is a geodesic of the sphere. Fix $(x,u) \in \T \calM$ with $||v||=1$ and let $c$ be given by the above equation. Clearly, $c(t) \in \Sd$ for all $t \in \reals$ as for $t \in \reals$ \begin{aligned} ||c(t)||^2 = \cos^2(t)||x||^2 + \sin^2(t)||v||^2 = \cos^2(t)+ \sin^2(t) = 1, \end{aligned} using that $u \in \text{span}(x)^{\perp}$. Further, $c$ is also clearly smooth as $\cos$ and $\sin$ are smooth. Finally, for $t \in \reals$ observe that \begin{aligned} c'(t) &= - \sin(t)x + \cos(t)v \text{ and thus } \\ \frac{d}{dt} c'(t) &= - \cos(t)x - \sin(t)v = - c(t). \end{aligned} As $\T_{c(t)} \calM = \text{span}(c(t))^{\perp}$ we get that \begin{aligned} c''(t) = \Proj_{c(t)} (\frac{d}{dt} c'(t)) = - \Proj_{c(t)} (c(t)) = 0, \end{aligned} where we used that as $\Sd$ is seen as a Riemannian submanifold, we have that $\frac{\D}{dt} c'(t) = \Proj_{c(t)} (\frac{d}{dt} c'(t))$.
